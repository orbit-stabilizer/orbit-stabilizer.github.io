My Thoughts On: Lambda World 2018 - The Complexity Trap

The Talk The Blog Post

Why do we add complexity to our projects?

One reason is that we have to. A 'Hello World' program is inherently less complex than an operating system. But is that the only reason? Fred Brooks, a Turing award winner, published a software engineering paper in 1986 titled, "No Silver Bullet - Essence and Accident in Software Engineering". In that paper he argues that there are two types of complexity: essential and accidental. Essential complexity is inherent to the problem domain, it cannot be overcome. Accidental complexity, on the other hand, is of a different sort. This second type of complexity is one that developers bring upon themselves and, in theory, should be possible to remove. Fred Brooks argues that accidental complexity has decreased significantly and we should not worry about it anymore. However, as the the talk points out, it is not 1986 anymore. Accidental complexity has increased significantly, as anyone who has worked in software development can probably attest to. There are a couple major ways we add this type of complexity to our projects.

The first is by embracing "industry standards". At this point, some of you might be saying, "Hold on a minute - what's wrong with industry standards?". Well, sometimes when we refer to industry standard, we do not mean tried and tested technologies. Rather, we refer to a fad, something that is currently hot and has not stood the test of time. There is nothing inherently wrong with fads, but it is important to be clear what we are referring to when we say industry standard. We tend to dismiss the boring solutions that work and we gravitate towards the new and shiny, sometimes to our own detriment, all the while hiding behind the masses, saying "This is how everyone else does it now!".

The second is by not considering trade-offs. What do we gain and what do we lose by adding abstractions or by not adding abstractions? Should we use this library if it will make our lives easier in the short-term, but will cause maintenance problems in the long run? Too often, we get tunnel visioned and do not consider the wider context.

To me, all of this can distilled down to the following question: How can we do this, why do we have to do it like that, and what are the tradeoffs between this and other approaches?

To end, I want to discuss how this played out personally with me. For a long time, I have wanted a blog - somewhere I can write my thoughts down. But, before I could do that, I needed to learn CSS, and not just CSS, but CSS frameworks because that is what I thought everyone used. Well, I learned CSS, but that was not enough. I had learned about build tools and Sass at work and so I thought I needed to use those in the creation of my blog before I could start writing for my blog. Well, I did that. But then, I learned React and thought I needed a fancy single page app, because that was currently cool. Then I realized, I am just going to be writing on my website, it is just a blog after all, so I have no need for React. But then, but then, I learned about static site generators - they seemed perfect for my task. But then, I had to decide which one to use... Gatsby.js so I could be using React? Hakyll because it might get me started learning Haskell?

Gah. At this point, I just threw up my hands. I was going to use HTML and a little bit of CSS, and I was going to put my blog up and start writing. To hell with all this other stuff - I would use it, if and when I actually needed to. And so, finally, here it is - my blog, exceptionally bland and blazingly fast.
